## The Story

While the empire built by Alexander the Great was still fragmenting into squabbling successor kingdoms, and Rome and Carthage were circling each other toward the wars that would define the Mediterranean, something opposite was happening on the other side of the world. Around 227 BCE, roughly 2250 years ago, ancient China was about to be unified for the first time — not through diplomacy, but through conquest.

One kingdom, Qin, was swallowing everything in sight — not just winning wars, but erasing nations entirely. Think of it like one European country conquering all of Europe one by one. Its king was Ying Zheng, one of the most ruthless rulers in history. He would later become Qin Shi Huang — the first emperor of a unified China.

The small kingdom of Yan was next on his list. Desperate, its crown prince hatched a plan: send an assassin named Jing Ke to the Qin court under the pretense of offering territory. The gift would include a map — and hidden inside the map, a poisoned dagger. Get close enough, unroll the map, grab the dagger, kill the king.

Jing Ke knew he probably wasn't coming back. Before he left, his friends saw him off at the Yi River. That's where he recited the poem — wind blowing, water cold, the strong man departs and will not return.

He made it to the Qin court. He unrolled the map. The dagger appeared — and this moment became a Chinese idiom still used today: **"圖窮匕見"** (the map ends and the dagger is revealed), meaning the moment a hidden intention is finally exposed.

He lunged at the king.

Missed. The king ran around a pillar. Here's the detail that makes the scene unforgettable: by court protocol, ministers were forbidden from carrying weapons in the king's presence. So the most powerful court in the world stood frozen by their own rules, watching helplessly, while the king of Qin ran for his life around a pillar.

The king eventually reached his sword, wounded Jing Ke, and killed him.

The assassination failed. Qin used it as justification to destroy Yan even faster. Ying Zheng went on to unify all of China and build an empire — which lasted fifteen years before collapsing.

The poem Jing Ke recited at the river has lasted 2250 years.

## 易水歌 (227 BCE)

**荊軻**

> 風蕭蕭兮易水寒，
> 壯士一去兮不復還。

---

## Translation

*— Anthony*

> Wind blow blow  
> Water cold cold  
> Strong man go go  
> Come back no no

**A note on this translation:** The original is not playful — it is a solemn poem about bravery and loyalty, a man facing certain death. This translation is intentional sabotage: word-play that flattens the gravity into something almost childish. Whether that's irreverent or illuminating is left to the reader.

## Discussion

Four lines. Twenty characters. A man saying goodbye to everyone he loves, knowing he will die.

Why has this poem survived 2250 years?
