*Class notes from April 13, 2026*

This week we explored how Tolstoy's philosophical sentences can be read as micro-poems.

## Why Can We Read These Sentences Like Poetry?

Tolstoy is not a poet in the traditional sense, but some of his sentences feel like micro-poems:
- Very short, but full of depth
- They don't rhyme; they rely on thought and rhythm of language
- They look like "life quotes," but they also push you to question yourself

In a poetry class, you can treat these as "prose poems" or "philosophical poems" and read them the way you read poetry.

## Four Examples

**1. "The value of life is not measured by time, but by depth."**

- *Language:* Contrast "time" and "depth"; "depth" is a spatial metaphor for how rich your experience is.
- *Discussion:* Can you think of a day that was short in time, but long in feeling?

**2. "Life is not a form of enjoyment, but a very heavy task."**

- *Language:* "Enjoyment vs. task" makes life feel like a responsibility.
- *Discussion:* If we change "task" to "journey" or "project," how does the feeling change?

**3. "There is only one time that is important, and that is now! It is important because it is the only time we can act."**

- *Language:* Repeated "now" and "act" turn the present moment into a moral question.
- *Discussion:* When did you fail to act in the "now"?

**4. "What takes away our peace is not a changing environment, but our insatiable desire."**

- *Language:* Contrast "environment" and "desire" moves the problem inside you.
- *Discussion:* Have you ever felt uneasy even when your life situation was good? Why?

## A Simple Way to Appreciate This Kind of Sentence

1. **Read it slowly, out loud:** Notice pauses and natural stresses like in poetry.
2. **Look at the structure:** Is there contrast? Metaphor? Repetition?
3. **Ask:** What does this sentence challenge me to think about?
4. **Attach your own experience:** Match it with one moment in your life.

## Writing Exercise: Create Your Own "Philosophical Line"

Write one or two lines about "life," "time," or "desire" in your own voice.

You can:
- Use contrast: "Not ..., but ..."
- Use metaphor: e.g., "Time is a river," "Desire is a storm"
- Ask a question: e.g., "What are we chasing that keeps us from living?"

**Goal:** One short, clear line that feels like a cross between a quote and a poem. Then share with the class and ask: "What question does this line ask you?"

## Class Notes & Student Poems

*From our April 13, 2026 class*

During class, we tried writing our own Tolstoy-style philosophical lines. Here are some student creations:

### Student Poems

> **Fish do not know water in water,**  
> **and people do not know the air / breath in.**

*The move:* What surrounds us completely becomes invisible. The fish doesn't know it's wet.

> **Our life is same like the Sun—**  
> **rise up and down.**

*Using metaphor to show life's cycles.*

> **A calm heart can cure three thousand ailments,**  
> **and a calm mind can understand the principle of all things.**

*An old Chinese proverb using parallelism—calm heart heals the body, calm mind comprehends truth.*

> **Nature is the best healing.**

> **One flower, one world; one leaf, one Bodhi.**

*A Buddhist saying (一花一世界，一葉一菩提): the tiny flowers and leaves contain infinite cosmic connections. Ordinary things contain the wisdom of enlightenment.*

> **Life is not a run.**  
> **It is a stroll on the trail,**  
> **smelling the fragrance of flowers.**

> **Desire is a well—it is wet and dark.**

> **Time is just time, but when was the charm?**
