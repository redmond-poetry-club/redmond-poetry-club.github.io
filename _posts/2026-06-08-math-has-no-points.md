## The Poem

> **Math has no points?**  
> **No, it often has points.**  
> Infinitely many —  
> Uncountably many,  
> And they are not the same.
>
> One could measure nothing  
> yet have infinitely many points —  
> uncountably many, to be exact.  
> **Just ask Cantor.**
>
> With choice,  
> we believe there is a point  
> in the product of non-empty.  
> Tarski shows us the magic  
> of doubling the sphere.
>
> The numbered Cantor armies.  
> All nowhere dense.  
> Yet their union  
> can never be complete —  
> **Baire commands.**
>
> Now tell me,  
> **Math has no points?**  
> **No, it has uncountably many.**

*— a student*

## References to Explore

The poem is packed with references to real mathematical concepts:

- **Cantor** — Georg Cantor proved that some infinities are larger than others. The real numbers are *uncountably* infinite, while the integers are merely *countably* infinite.
- **Tarski** — The Banach–Tarski paradox shows that a solid sphere can be decomposed and reassembled into *two* identical spheres, using the Axiom of Choice.
- **Baire** — The Baire category theorem tells us that even uncountably many "nowhere dense" sets (like the Cantor set) cannot cover all of the real line.
