## About Cinco de Mayo

Cinco de Mayo is **not** Mexican Independence Day — that is September 16th. It marks a single 1862 battle (the Battle of Puebla).

The holiday as Americans know it was largely invented in California by Mexican-Americans. Mexicans don't mind an American-arranged party that raises awareness of their culture. And when they are a few drinks in, they sing something like this — loud, proud, a little macho.

This song is from the Robert Rodriguez film *Desperado*, sung by Antonio Banderas. Mexicans adopted it immediately.

## What Is Interesting

### Mi morena
Dark-haired, dark-skinned woman. A term of endearment in Mexican culture — beauty *is* morena. The Virgin of Guadalupe is morena. The English "dark-haired girl" loses almost everything.

### The ay ay ay
Not sadness — **overflow**. A feeling too big for words collapsing into sound. Mexicans sing this chorus loudly, together, drinks in hand. It is designed for a crowd.

### Las mujeres / mi amor
The verse boasts: women never lack him, *plural*. Then the chorus collapses into one: *mi amor, mi morena, de mi corazón*. The bravado is the public face. The tenderness is underneath.

### Aguardiente
Literally "firewater" — *agua* (water) + *ardiente* (burning). They named their drink "burning water." The word itself is a tiny poem, and the whole attitude of the song in two syllables.

### Jineteando
Not just "riding" — a gerund modifying *me voy* (I go). "Riding my horse, I head into the mountains." One motion. The riding and the going are the same thing, compressed.

### Simple and unapologetic
Horse, guitar, tequila, woman. No metaphor, no hidden meaning. The joy is on the surface. After Tagore's compressed depth, this is the opposite move. **Both are honest.**
