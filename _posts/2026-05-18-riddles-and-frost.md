This week we explored the playful wisdom of Anglo-Saxon riddles and the deceptive simplicity of Robert Frost's most famous poem.

## Anglo-Saxon Riddles

*Translated by Richard Hamer*  
*Source: The Norton Anthology of Poetry (1996)*

### Historical Background

The Anglo-Saxons were Germanic tribes—Angles, Saxons, and Jutes—who migrated from what is now Germany and Denmark to Britain after the Roman withdrawal (5th–6th century). They brought their language (Old English), warrior culture, and oral poetry traditions. The Exeter Book, where these riddles survive, was compiled around 970 AD—a rare window into a world where poetry was riddle, prayer, and battle-song all at once.

The Anglo-Saxons loved riddles. These three come from the Exeter Book, a 10th-century manuscript. Each riddle personifies an object, challenging us to see ordinary things anew.

### Riddle 1

> I am a lonely being, scarred by swords,  
> Wounded by iron, sated with battle-deeds,  
> Wearied by blades. Often I witness war,  
> Perilous fight, nor hope for consolation,  
> That any help may rescue me from strife  
> Before I perish among fighting men;  
> But hammered swords, hard edged and grimly sharp,  
> Batter me, and the handwork of the smith  
> Bites in the castles; I must ever wait  
> A contest yet more cruel. I could never  
> In any habitation find the sort  
> Of doctor who could heal my wounds with herbs;  
> But cuts from swords ever increase on me  
> Through deadly contest, both by day and night.

**Answer:** *A Shield*

### Riddle 2

> My dress is silent when I tread the ground  
> Or stay at home or stir upon the waters.  
> Sometimes my trappings and the lofty air  
> Raise me above the dwelling-place of men,  
> And then the power of clouds carries me far  
> Above the people; and my ornaments  
> Loudly resound, send forth a melody  
> And clearly sing, when I am not in touch  
> With earth or water, but a flying spirit.

**Traditional answer:** *A Swan*

**Student's interpretation:** *A Paddle* — The splashed water is the "dress," silent when still; the paddle pushes forward in the water, and when lifted out, it resembles the notion of flight, singing through the air.

### Riddle 3

> A moth ate words; a marvelous event  
> I thought it when I heard about that wonder,  
> A worm had swallowed some man's lay*, a thief  
> In darkness had consumed the mighty saying  
> With its foundation firm. The thief was not  
> One whit the wiser when he ate those words.

*\*A "lay" is a short poem intended to be sung.*

**Traditional answer:** *A Bookworm*

**Student's interpretation:** *Time* — What else steals knowledge from a person, not by force, but silently? Memory fades with time. The words, the poems, consumed in darkness — and time is no wiser for having taken them.

## The Road Not Taken
*Robert Frost (1916)*

> Two roads diverged in a yellow wood,  
> And sorry I could not travel both  
> And be one traveler, long I stood  
> And looked down one as far as I could  
> To where it bent in the undergrowth;  
>
> Then took the other, as just as fair,  
> And having perhaps the better claim,  
> Because it was grassy and wanted wear;  
> Though as for that the passing there  
> Had worn them really about the same,  
>
> And both that morning equally lay  
> In leaves no step had trodden black.  
> Oh, I kept the first for another day!  
> Yet knowing how way leads on to way,  
> I doubted if I should ever come back.  
>
> I shall be telling this with a sigh  
> Somewhere ages and ages hence:  
> Two roads diverged in a wood, and I—  
> I took the one less traveled by,  
> And that has made all the difference.

### Line-by-Line Commentary

| Line | Commentary |
|------|------------|
| *Two roads diverged in a yellow wood* | The fork in the road symbolizes life choices. The autumn setting suggests maturity, transition, and reflection. |
| *And sorry I could not travel both* | The speaker regrets that life allows only one path at a time. |
| *And be one traveler, long I stood* | He hesitates carefully before making a decision. |
| *And looked down one as far as I could* | He tries to foresee where one choice may lead. |
| *To where it bent in the undergrowth* | The future eventually disappears from view. |
| *Then took the other, as just as fair* | The second road initially seems equally attractive. |
| *And having perhaps the better claim* | The word "perhaps" reveals uncertainty. |
| *Because it was grassy and wanted wear* | The speaker thinks it may be slightly less traveled. |
| *Though as for that the passing there* | He immediately begins revising his earlier assumption. |
| *Had worn them really about the same* | Both roads were actually very similar. |
| *And both that morning equally lay* | Neither road was truly more traveled. |
| *In leaves no step had trodden black* | The untouched leaves emphasize freshness and possibility. |
| *Oh, I kept the first for another day!* | He comforts himself with the idea of returning later. |
| *Yet knowing how way leads on to way* | One decision leads to another, shaping a life path. |
| *I doubted if I should ever come back* | He realizes most choices are irreversible. |
| *I shall be telling this with a sigh* | The sigh may express nostalgia, regret, or irony. |
| *Somewhere ages and ages hence* | The speaker imagines retelling the story far in the future. |
| *Two roads diverged in a wood, and I—* | The dramatic pause emphasizes the importance of the decision. |
| *I took the one less traveled by* | Ironically, the poem earlier said the roads were nearly identical. |
| *And that has made all the difference* | The ending remains ambiguous: the difference may be good, bad, or mixed. |

### The Irony We Miss

Robert Frost's "The Road Not Taken" is often interpreted as a celebration of individuality and brave decision-making. However, the poem is much more subtle and ironic than it first appears. Frost repeatedly emphasizes that the two roads were actually very similar, yet the speaker later imagines himself describing one as "less traveled."

This suggests that human beings often create meaningful stories about their past choices in order to give shape and purpose to their lives.

The poem explores:
- **Uncertainty** — we rarely know which choice is "right"
- **Regret** — we wonder about paths not taken
- **Memory** — we reshape the past to fit our narratives
- **Self-deception** — we claim uniqueness where none existed
- **The impossibility of living multiple lives at once**

Its elegance comes from Frost's conversational language combined with deep philosophical reflection beneath the surface.

**Student's interpretation:** The road less traveled—a life choice made by fewer people—doesn't necessarily mean better or worse. The poem says nothing about that either. It merely made *a difference*. Not "a better life," not "a worse life"—just a different one.
